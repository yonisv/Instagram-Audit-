Brand 01 · The Trailblazer

Red Bull

"Watch the World Of Red Bull" · @redbull

10 /10 Elite
32.7MFollowers
11,410Posts
2,128Following
Media Co.Strategy

I want to start here because Red Bull is the reason this audit exists. Before you study any other brand's Instagram, you need to understand what Red Bull did — because they didn't just get good at social media, they redefined what a brand account can be.

Notice their bio says "Watch the World Of Red Bull." Not "Try our energy drink." They don't sell the product on Instagram — they sell a worldview. Landing a plane on a moving train. A helicopter doing backflips. Athletes pushing the edge of what's possible. The can shows up occasionally, but it's never the point.

What Red Bull did was build a media company inside a beverage brand. They have Red Bull Media House producing documentaries, films, and original content. Instagram is just one distribution channel in a much bigger ecosystem. With 11,410 posts, they've been operating at the pace of a news organization for years.

The caption writing alone is a masterclass — "what happened here? a) too steep b) the brakes did not work c) took the wrong way." That multiple-choice format generates comments without begging for engagement. Every hook is engineered to stop the scroll. This is the gold standard. Everything else in this audit is measured against Red Bull.

Brand 02 · Best Community Voice

Dunkin'

"Instagram runs on Dunkin'" · @dunkin

8 /10 Strong
2.9MFollowers
2,985Posts
6,046Following
Meme-firstStrategy

Dunkin' is doing something most legacy food brands can't pull off — they sound like they're run by actual Gen Z humans who use the internet, not a social media agency presenting monthly reports. Every post feels written by someone who genuinely enjoys being online, and that authenticity is the entire strategy.

The "Dirty Soda" campaign is a perfect example. "A dirty f**kin' drink," "send this to someone who did you dirty," "if you like dirty stuff." They're using word-censorship as a creative device — people fill in the blank themselves, which drives engagement without Dunkin having to ask for it. That's platform-native thinking.

Their bio — "Instagram runs on Dunkin'" — turns a 70-year-old tagline into a platform-aware joke. That's not a coincidence. Someone at Dunkin' understands that the medium is part of the message.

Where do they lose points? The content is almost entirely meme-format stills. They're leaving a significant Reels opportunity on the table, and there's room to push even further with video given how strong their written voice already is.

Brand 03 · Biggest Gap

Chick-fil-A

"Chicken. Joy. Delivered." · @chickfila

3 /10 Underperforming
2.1MFollowers
1,772Posts
266Following
CorporateStrategy

This one hurts because Chick-fil-A has more untapped Instagram potential than any brand in this audit. Every teenager and young adult in America has a relationship with this brand. The sauce has memes. The "my pleasure" response has memes. The Sunday closings have memes. None of that cultural capital is showing up on their Instagram.

What you see instead is a corporate bulletin board. Employee spotlights. Scholarship announcements. Product shots approved by three layers of legal. The photography is actually beautiful — clean, well-lit, professional — but completely passive. It talks at you instead of with you.

The most damning number: they're following only 266 accounts. That tells you everything. They're broadcasting, not listening. They're not part of the conversation — they're standing next to it.

The content writes itself. "My pleasure" moments. Waffle fry content. Behind-the-counter culture. The chaos of a Monday morning drive-through. Someone else is making all that content and getting the engagement while Chick-fil-A posts an employee photo. This is the biggest gap between brand love and Instagram execution I've ever seen.

Brand 04 · Premium Mismatch

Celsius

"CELSIUS. LIVE. FIT. GO." · @celsiusofficial

5 /10 Average
1.1MFollowers
3,113Posts
179Following
Events-firstStrategy

Celsius is arguably the number two energy drink in the United States by retail sales. So when you see 1.1 million followers, that number is almost insulting relative to their actual market position.

The Instagram is genuinely well-executed visually. Clean product shots, strong teal/cyan color system, event coverage from the Celsius Soccer Classic. They're buying lifestyle credibility through events and the feed reflects that premium positioning.

But here's my core critique: they're winning at retail, not at community. People buy Celsius at Target and the gym but aren't following the page. That disconnect tells me Celsius hasn't figured out how to convert buyers into followers. The Instagram feels made for people who already know the brand, not for discovery.

They also follow only 179 accounts — the lowest of any brand here. For a brand at their scale, 1.1M followers should be 5M+. The premium events strategy is smart for positioning but comes at the cost of the organic growth that community engagement drives.

Brand 05 · Beautiful But Passive

Owala

"Where Boring Ends." · @owala

6 /10 Good, Not Great
1MFollowers
1,010Posts
102Following
Product catalogStrategy

Owala's Instagram is the most visually satisfying of everything in this audit. Hot pink on salmon. Neon yellow against warm tan. Deep navy with chrome accents. The color work is genuinely exceptional — every product shot looks like a still from a fashion editorial. If Instagram was judged on aesthetics alone, Owala ranks second behind Red Bull.

The bio is sharp too: "Where Boring Ends." Three words that position them against Stanley and Hydro Flask without naming either. Their 1M followers from 1,010 posts is a better follower-to-post ratio than almost anyone else here — every piece of content is earning its keep.

Here's the tension: Owala has cult status on TikTok. The "Owala obsession" content is everywhere — people filming their collections, doing unboxings, carrying them daily. That community is being built by consumers, not by Owala themselves. The Instagram is essentially a product catalog. Beautiful, intentional — but passive.

The fans are already there. They just need to be invited in. If Owala leaned into the community the way their customers already behave, this could be a 5M+ account inside of two years.

Brand 06 · Right Fit, Right Audience

Costa Sunglasses

"Born on the water in 1983." · @costasunglasses

7 /10 Well-Calibrated
565KFollowers
3,954Posts
494Following
StorytellingStrategy

I'm going to say something counterintuitive: Costa's 565K followers is not a weakness. It's evidence of precision. Their target customer is not a 19-year-old scrolling through memes. It's a 42-year-old who wakes up at 4:30am to be on the water before sunrise. That person doesn't use Instagram the same way.

The bio — "Born on the water in 1983. For those who need water to breathe." — is identity language. Not describing a product. Describing a person. If that sentence resonates with you, you're the customer. If it doesn't, Costa doesn't want your follow anyway. That kind of intentional audience exclusion is a sign of genuine brand maturity.

The content is cinematic and patient. Long rods and tarpon jumping. Mothers and sons tying flies together. Open ocean swells from the deck of a boat. The pacing is slow by Instagram standards, but it's perfect for an audience that values the meditative quality of being on the water.

Those 565K followers are buying $200 sunglasses repeatedly, tagging Costa in their catches, and advocating to their fishing buddies. The right 565K beats the wrong 2M every time.

Final Analysis

What This Audit Actually Teaches Us

After six brands, six strategies, and six honest grades — a few things become undeniable. Instagram success isn't universal. It's contextual.

01

Don't sell the product — sell the world

Red Bull almost never talks about energy drinks. They talk about what's possible when you have the energy. The product is subtext. The world is the text.

02

Sound like your audience, not your legal team

Dunkin' wins because every caption sounds like it was written by someone who loves being online. Chick-fil-A loses because every caption feels approved by three VPs.

03

Follower count is the wrong metric

Costa has 565K followers and one of the most loyal customer bases in their category. Chick-fil-A has 2.1M and near-zero engagement. Quality beats quantity every time.

04

Beautiful content ≠ community building

Owala's feed is stunning. Their community is being built by consumers on TikTok without their help. Great aesthetics and great strategy are two different things.

05

Market position ≠ social success

Celsius is #2 in energy drinks by retail sales. They have 1.1M Instagram followers. Those two facts have almost nothing to do with each other.

06

Know how your audience actually uses the internet

Costa's customer watches 45-minute fishing videos on YouTube. Dunkin's scrolls memes at 11pm. Same platform, completely different behavior. The best brands understand this.
